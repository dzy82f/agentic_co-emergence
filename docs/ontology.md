# Agentic Co-Emergence Ontology

This document defines the canonical vocabulary of the Agentic Co-Emergence research programme.

The purpose of the ontology is to establish precise definitions for the concepts used throughout the theory, the runtime, the experiments, and the Living Paper.

Definitions are expected to evolve as the research programme matures. Changes should occur deliberately and be reflected consistently across the repository.

---

# Understanding

**Working Definition**

Understanding is a structured, evolving, purpose-relative state that organises concepts, relationships, assumptions, evidence, uncertainties, and tensions within a domain, thereby constraining interpretation, inference, and action.

Understanding is not the possession of information or facts. Rather, it is the organised capacity to interpret a domain, relate ideas, recognise uncertainty, revise assumptions, and guide possible action.

Within Agentic Co-Emergence, understanding is treated as a first-class computational object.

### Core Properties

* Identity
* Structure
* State
* Domain Context
* History
* Transformability
* Perspective
* Uncertainty
* Tension
* Purpose

---

# Knowledge

Knowledge consists of propositions, observations, experiences, or models that are considered sufficiently reliable for use within a particular context.

Knowledge contributes to understanding but is not equivalent to it.

A system may possess extensive knowledge while exhibiting little understanding if that knowledge is poorly organised or cannot be meaningfully applied.

---

# Information

Information is data interpreted within a context.

Information reduces uncertainty about some aspect of a domain but does not by itself constitute knowledge or understanding.

Information is an input to the evolution of understanding.

---

# Dialogue

Dialogue is a governed process through which participants exchange, challenge, refine, and integrate perspectives.

Within Agentic Co-Emergence, dialogue is viewed not primarily as communication but as a sequence of transformations applied to one or more understanding states.

---

# Transformation

A transformation is any operation that changes an understanding.

Examples include:

* incorporating new evidence;
* identifying assumptions;
* resolving contradictions;
* introducing new concepts;
* revising relationships;
* increasing or decreasing confidence;
* reframing a problem.

Transformations are the fundamental mechanism by which understanding evolves.

---

# Agent

An agent is an autonomous participant capable of contributing to the evolution of understanding.

An agent may be:

* human;
* artificial;
* organisational; or
* institutional.

The defining characteristic of an agent is not its implementation but its ability to participate in governed transformations of understanding.

---

# Co-Emergence

Co-Emergence is the process through which multiple participants collectively produce an understanding that cannot be attributed solely to any individual participant.

Co-Emergence is characterised by iterative interaction, mutual adaptation, and the progressive reorganisation of shared understanding.

The resulting understanding may differ qualitatively from the initial understandings contributed by individual participants.

---

# Governance

Governance comprises the rules, constraints, protocols, and decision processes that regulate how understanding may evolve.

Governance exists to preserve integrity, transparency, traceability, and accountability throughout the process of co-emergence.

Governance does not determine conclusions; it determines the legitimacy of the process by which conclusions are reached.

---

# Computational Object

A computational object is an entity that possesses an identity, an internal structure, observable state, defined operations, and a history of change.

Within Agentic Co-Emergence, understanding is proposed to be a computational object.

This hypothesis underpins the development of the runtime and is subject to empirical validation.

---

# Domain

A domain is the bounded area of inquiry within which understanding is developed.

Understanding is always relative to a domain.

No understanding exists independently of some context or problem space.

---

# Perspective

A perspective is a coherent interpretation of a domain arising from a particular participant, role, experience, or conceptual framework.

Different perspectives may coexist even when they appear contradictory.

Dialogue provides the mechanism through which perspectives interact during co-emergence.

---

# Tension

A tension is an unresolved incompatibility within an understanding.

Tensions are not necessarily errors.

They frequently represent opportunities for deeper inquiry, conceptual innovation, or theoretical revision.

Maintaining explicit awareness of tensions is considered essential to the evolution of understanding.

---

# Uncertainty

Uncertainty represents recognised limits to current understanding.

It may arise from incomplete evidence, conflicting interpretations, ambiguous concepts, or insufficient analysis.

Explicit representation of uncertainty is regarded as a strength rather than a weakness of an understanding.

---

# Purpose

Purpose defines the objective against which an understanding is evaluated.

Examples include:

* explanation;
* prediction;
* decision-making;
* design;
* governance;
* discovery.

Purpose determines what constitutes improvement within a particular understanding.

---

# Notes

This ontology is intended to evolve alongside the Agentic Co-Emergence theory, the runtime implementation, and the Living Paper.

Definitions should remain internally consistent, computationally meaningful, and empirically testable wherever possible.
